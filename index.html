The Story of Cezium Games

One of my earliest gaming memories is playing Lemmings in the mid-1990s. I was maybe five years old, but I was completely fascinated. Games weren't just something to pass the time—they were puzzles, adventures, and worlds that invited you to explore. I spent my childhood playing anything I could get my hands on, never imagining that one day I'd be building games of my own.

Fast forward years later, and I went to college to study physics, eventually specializing in astrophysics. During my final year, though, I realized something important: I loved solving problems, but I didn't want to spend my career in a laboratory. I wanted to create things.

I decided to learn how to make games. The only problem was that I had absolutely no idea how.

I had no programming background beyond my science coursework, no artistic experience, no design training—nothing. I picked up Python, started experimenting, and learned everything through trial and error.

Around that same time I completed my senior thesis, which focused on gamification and how the principles that make games engaging can also make learning more effective. As part of that project I created my first game, Cosmic Horizons. It was light, but it was a great first attempt at something and it got me through my thesis.

My next project, Shadow Quest, was an ambitious JRPG that was wildly beyond my abilities at the time. I spent nearly a year pouring almost every free hour into it. I filled notebooks with sketches, mechanics, maps, and ideas, teaching myself programming, design, balance, and production as I went.

Looking back, it's rough, but it's also likely the project I'm most proud of — knowing so little and yet building so much, when I go back and look at it I almost can't believe what I built.

As I was finishing up Shadow Quest, I got a job at Riot Games and that's when I really got a dose of making games. As a longtime League of Legends player, joining Riot gave me an incredible opportunity to work alongside some of the best game designers in the industry. I helped evaluate gameplay balance, champion design, and player experience, gaining firsthand insight into how great games are built, refined, and polished.

Those years fundamentally changed how I think about design. They taught me that great games aren't defined by complexity—they're defined by clarity, accessibility, and thousands of thoughtful decisions that make every interaction feel meaningful.

After Riot, I returned to independent development with a project that was much more personal. My girlfriend at that time loved pugs, and we had an incredible pug named Peach who inspired Pug Time. What began as a fun passion project gradually became my first commercial game. After months of development, I launched a Kickstarter campaign to self-publish it.

It reached its funding goal — something that I was constantly told would never happen throughout the process — but I'm accustomed to rejecting expectations.

After Pug Time, I began to explore two other games I had dreamed about making.

For the first, I began exploring Cyborg Defense, a roguelike base-defense game built around an image that had been stuck in my head for years: a lone cyborg defending the last survivors of humanity in a ruined city.

Shortly after, I started designing Elemental Fury, a cooperative tabletop game where players wield the power of the elements to take down towering bosses together. Inspired by some of my favorite cooperative games, it became a project that continually evolved as my understanding of game design grew.

Then I joined Epic Games, where I once again had the opportunity to learn from incredibly talented developers. My personal projects naturally slowed down, but they never stopped. Between work and life, I kept returning to them whenever inspiration struck.

Over time, Elemental Fury became my most polished game (design-wise). Every game I've built has taught me something, but this one represents years of lessons coming together. I genuinely believe it stands alongside the very best cooperative tabletop games I've played, and it's a project I continue to refine today.

Beyond those bigger explorations, I also dug into small games as little side projects and gifts for my family:

  • The Garden Game was created for my mom.
  • Don't Get Glutened was built as a gift for my wife—a silly party game inspired by the adventures (and occasional mishaps) that come with living gluten-free.
  • Tornado Run draws inspiration from growing up in Kansas and the fascination that comes with tornados.

At its heart, Cezium Games exists because I love people and the joy I get to provide by creating something beautiful.

A great game gives people permission to disconnect from everything else for a little while. It creates moments where families laugh together, friends challenge each other, or someone simply loses themselves in a world that's more fun than whatever stress they're carrying that day.

I believe the best games are approachable without being shallow, easy to learn without sacrificing depth, and welcoming to everyone who picks them up.

That's the story of Cezium Games.
